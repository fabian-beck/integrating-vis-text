Introduction

Similar but different

A data visualization is a visual representation of information encoded in geometric primitives that we perceive, process, and interpret to consume the information.

A text is a visual representation of information encoded in characters that we perceive, process, and interpret to consume the information.

Integrated visualization and text can make a difference

The way of encoding might be different but a visualization and a text are hence similar in the sense that they both convey information to a human reader. Often, they are combined and complement each other as part of documents, user interfaces, presentation slides and the like. While this complementing similarity is certainly not a new observation, it still remains surprising to me how little the combination and close integration of the two modalities haven been investigated. I believe we can do much more than just placing the visualization next to the text, like in a traditional research paper or printed news article. With this book, I want to explore possibilities for a better integration:

This is also the outline of the book.
  • How graphics at the size of word can be embedded in line with the text.
  • How interactive documents can connect visualizations and related text fragments.
  • How text that is automatically generated integrates with visualization.
  • How other types of text such as source code becomes visually agumentated.
  • How interactive systems might leverage integrated designs of text and viualization.
Is the book for you?

This book is written for everybody being interested in how to effectively communicate data and information as part of documents beyond what is the state of the art currently. The book is not targeted at novelists and poets, but at authors of data-related texts such as data reports, papers, and news stories, at designers and developers of user interfaces and authoring systems, and at researchers from diverse fields not limited to human-computer interaction, visualization, scientific communication, data-driven journalism, and media science. I am a computer scientist and visualization researcher, hence, the book has a certain technical and a specific research perspective, but I am trying to address a broader scope.

Text Generation

Natural Language Generation is an established field providing many techniques to produce text from data. In a way, it is the little sister of Natural Language Processing, that is, the extraction of data from natural language text. In context of Siri, Alexa, Cortana, and other chat bots everybody talks about their abilities to understand and interpret natural language, but they actually also need to answer and generate natural language. Since our examples are documents, here, I focus only on the generation of the written text, but not how a computer can read and pronounce the words in a natural way.
